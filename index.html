FAQs

Common D&O Insurance Questions

What is D&O insurance and do I need it as a director?

Directors and Officers (D&O) insurance protects you personally against claims made against you in your capacity as a director or officer of a company. Under the Companies Act 1993, directors can be held personally liable for wrongful acts, reckless trading, and breaches of director duties. If you are a director of any company — large or small — D&O cover is strongly recommended to protect your personal assets.

How much does D&O insurance cost?

D&O insurance costs vary depending on the company's size, industry, revenue, and risk profile. For a small private company with revenue under $5M, basic D&O cover can start from around NZ$800–$1,500 per year. For listed companies or complex risks, premiums can reach tens of thousands of dollars annually. A broker can provide precise quotes based on your specific situation.

What is the difference between D&O and management liability insurance?

D&O insurance primarily covers individual directors and officers for personal liability. Management liability (ML) insurance is a broader package that bundles D&O cover with employment practices liability (EPL), crime cover, fiduciary liability, and statutory liability into a single policy. ML cover is typically more comprehensive and better suited to companies that want to protect both the entity and its management team.

Does D&O insurance cover criminal acts or fraud?

No. D&O insurance specifically excludes fraudulent, criminal, or deliberately dishonest acts. The insurance is designed to protect directors who acted in good faith but made decisions that later resulted in claims. If a court finds that a director acted fraudulently or criminally, the policy will not respond. Some policies include a "prior to final adjudication" clause that pays defence costs until fraud is proven.

As a non-executive director, am I personally liable for board decisions?

Yes. Under the Companies Act 1993, non-executive directors (NEDs) carry the same personal liability as executive directors. Courts have found that NEDs cannot simply rely on management's assurances — they have an active duty to make informed decisions and to question information presented to them. This makes personal D&O cover essential for all NEDs, regardless of their level of involvement in day-to-day operations.
