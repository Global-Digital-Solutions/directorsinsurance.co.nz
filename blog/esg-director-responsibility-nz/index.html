ESG and Director Responsibility: Climate Liability and TCFD for NZ Boards

Environmental, social and governance (ESG) responsibilities are creating real legal liability for NZ directors. TCFD reporting, climate litigation, and greenwashing claims are the new frontier of D&O risk.

By DirectorsInsurance Editorial·5 May 2026·8 min read

ESG Is Now a Director Liability Issue

Environmental, Social and Governance (ESG) was once a voluntary corporate responsibility framework. In New Zealand, it has become a legal obligation for some companies and a governance expectation for all of them. For directors, this shift has material implications for their personal liability.

The Financial Sector (Climate-related Disclosures and Other Matters) Amendment Act 2021 introduced mandatory climate-related disclosures for large NZX-listed companies, large registered banks, large insurers, and large investment managers. Directors of these entities are responsible for the accuracy and completeness of their climate disclosures — and face significant personal liability if those disclosures are misleading or materially incomplete.

TCFD: The Framework That Matters for Directors

The Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD) framework is the basis for New Zealand's mandatory climate disclosure requirements. Under the External Reporting Board's (XRB) standards, in-scope entities must disclose across four areas:

Governance: How the board and management oversee climate-related risks and opportunities. This disclosure is specifically director-level — it requires the board to articulate its own oversight processes, not just management's.

Strategy: The actual and potential impacts of climate-related risks and opportunities on the entity's businesses, strategy, and financial planning.

Risk Management: How the entity identifies, assesses, and manages climate-related risks.

Metrics and Targets: The metrics and targets used to assess and manage climate-related risks and opportunities.

Director Liability for Climate Disclosures

For directors of in-scope entities, misleading or false climate disclosures create the same liability exposure as misleading financial disclosures. The FMA has indicated it will treat climate disclosure violations seriously.

The specific director liability risks include:

  • Making materially false or misleading climate disclosures
  • Failing to disclose known material climate risks that affect investment decisions
  • Approving a corporate strategy that is inconsistent with disclosed climate commitments

Greenwashing: A Growing D&O Claim Risk

Greenwashing — making false or misleading claims about environmental credentials or sustainability practices — is an emerging source of director liability in NZ and globally.

The Commerce Commission has signalled increased enforcement focus on environmental claims under the Fair Trading Act 1986. Directors who approve marketing communications, product labelling, or investor communications that overstate environmental credentials face personal liability exposure.

Internationally, greenwashing class actions against company boards are now a reality. Litigation funders are actively building greenwashing cases. NZ directors of companies in energy, agriculture, property, manufacturing, and financial services should consider this exposure carefully.

ESG Beyond Climate: Social and Governance Dimensions

While climate is currently the highest-profile ESG liability area, directors also face growing exposure from:

Supply chain labour standards: The growing scrutiny of modern slavery and labour practices in supply chains creates due diligence obligations that NZ directors need to understand and act on.

Board diversity and inclusion: While not yet a legal requirement in NZ, institutional investors and major shareholders increasingly scrutinise board composition. Directors who ignore diversity obligations in governance documents may face shareholder pressure and accountability.

Remuneration: Executive pay decisions, particularly those seen as excessive against company performance, are a recurring source of shareholder activism and proxy challenges that can create personal accountability for the board.

Does D&O Insurance Cover ESG Claims?

Standard D&O insurance covers claims arising from directors' governance decisions — including decisions relating to ESG. Climate disclosure claims, greenwashing allegations that target directors personally, and regulatory investigations under the climate disclosure regime should fall within the standard D&O insuring clause.

However, some D&O policies contain pollution exclusions that may be read broadly enough to affect climate-related claims. Specialist brokers can review your policy wording to identify any potential coverage issues.

What Boards Should Do Now

Whether or not your company is subject to mandatory TCFD reporting, ESG governance has become a director responsibility. As a minimum, NZ boards should:

  • Understand whether the company is in-scope for mandatory climate disclosures
  • Review the board's climate oversight processes and document them clearly
  • Audit the company's public ESG and sustainability claims for accuracy
  • Ensure the board understands the regulatory enforcement landscape
  • Review D&O insurance cover for ESG-related claim scenarios
