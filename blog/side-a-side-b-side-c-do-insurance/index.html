Side A, Side B, Side C: The Complete Guide to D&O Policy Structure

Most directors know they have D&O insurance, but few understand how it actually works. This guide explains Side A, B, and C coverage and why the distinction matters when a real claim arrives.

By DirectorsInsurance Editorial·1 May 2026·7 min read

Why D&O Policy Structure Matters

D&O insurance is not a single, simple product. Most D&O policies — particularly those placed for larger private companies, public companies, and not-for-profit organisations — are structured into distinct coverage sides that respond to different claim scenarios.

Understanding which side of your D&O policy responds to a given claim is essential. Getting it wrong — or having inadequate coverage on the side that matters — can leave directors personally exposed at exactly the wrong time.

The Three Sides of D&O Insurance

Side A: Non-Indemnified Director Coverage

What it covers: Side A is the most important coverage for individual directors. It pays directly to directors when the company cannot or will not indemnify them — protecting their personal assets when the corporate shield is absent.

When it responds:

  • The company is insolvent and cannot fund the director's defence
  • Applicable law or regulation prohibits the company from indemnifying the director
  • The board of the company refuses to indemnify a director (for example, where the claim is by the company against the director)
  • The company's indemnification is subject to conditions the director cannot meet

Side A is personal director protection — it is what stands between a director and losing their personal assets when the company cannot protect them. In insolvency scenarios (where D&O claims are most likely), Side A is often the only protection available.

Key consideration: Every director should understand whether their D&O policy provides adequate Side A limits. In many bundled management liability policies, Side A, B, and C share a single aggregate limit — meaning a large Side B claim could exhaust the limit before Side A is called on.

Side B: Company Indemnification Reimbursement

What it covers: Side B reimburses the company after it has indemnified its directors. When the company defends a director, pays a settlement, or satisfies a judgment, Side B pays the company back.

When it responds:

  • A shareholder claims against a director for breach of duty, and the company defends and settles the claim
  • A regulatory authority investigates a director, and the company funds the defence
  • An employment claim is brought against a director personally, and the company funds the defence and settlement

Side B is the most frequently triggered coverage in practice — companies regularly indemnify their directors, and Side B is what reimburses them. Without adequate Side B, every director defence claim creates a direct cost to the company's balance sheet.

Side C: Entity Coverage (Company Coverage)

What it covers: Side C covers the company entity itself (not the directors personally) for certain claims — primarily securities claims in listed company contexts.

When it responds:

  • A securities class action names both the company and its directors — Side C covers the company's share of the claim
  • The company itself is a defendant in a shareholder action relating to securities disclosures

Side C is typically only relevant for listed companies. For private companies, entity coverage is less common and is sometimes addressed through other policy extensions.

Important note: Side C shares the aggregate limit with Sides A and B in most standard policies. A large securities class action naming the company can exhaust the aggregate limit, leaving nothing for directors' personal claims under Side A. This is why large listed companies typically purchase a dedicated Side A tower with separate limits.

The Side A DIC Tower: Essential for Listed Companies

For NZX-listed companies and large private companies with significant litigation exposure, D&O programmes often include a Side A DIC (Difference in Conditions) tower — a separate policy structure that:

  • Provides additional Side A limits above the primary policy aggregate
  • Responds where the primary policy does not respond (difference in conditions)
  • Cannot be eroded by Side B or Side C claims
  • Protects individual directors even if the primary programme is fully consumed by entity claims

The Side A DIC tower is the gold standard of personal director protection for listed company directors in NZ.

How Much of Each Side Do You Need?

For SME private companies:

A bundled management liability policy with a shared aggregate limit of NZ$2M–$5M is typically appropriate. The primary risk is employment claims (Side B / EPL) and regulatory investigations (statutory liability). Ensure the aggregate limit is adequate for the company's size.

For larger private companies and pre-IPO companies:

Consider separate Side A cover to ensure personal director protection is not eroded by entity claims. Increase aggregate limits to NZ$5M–$20M depending on revenue, employee count, and investor structure.

For NZX-listed companies:

A three-side primary programme plus a Side A DIC tower is standard. Aggregate limits for the primary programme of NZ$20M–$100M depending on market cap, plus a Side A DIC tower of NZ$10M–$50M.

For charities and incorporated societies:

A combined trustees' D&O policy with a single aggregate limit of NZ$1M–$5M is appropriate for most organisations.
