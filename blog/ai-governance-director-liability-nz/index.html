AI Governance and Director Liability in New Zealand: What Every Board Needs to Know

Artificial intelligence is creating a new category of director liability in NZ. As AI is deployed across finance, healthcare, and operations, boards that fail to govern it face regulatory and legal exposure.

By DirectorsInsurance Editorial·6 May 2026·9 min read

The AI Governance Imperative for NZ Boards

Artificial intelligence is no longer an emerging technology — it is deployed across financial services, healthcare, legal, insurance, and almost every major sector in New Zealand. For directors, this creates a new and rapidly evolving area of governance responsibility that the Institute of Directors NZ identified as a top-priority theme for 2025 and beyond.

The question for directors is no longer whether AI is relevant to their company — it is whether their board has the governance framework, expertise, and oversight processes to manage AI responsibly. Directors who fail to govern AI risks expose themselves to personal liability just as they would for any other material business risk they overlooked.

Why AI Creates Director Liability

Breach of Duty to Manage Risk

Under the Companies Act 1993, directors must exercise reasonable care, diligence, and skill in managing the company's affairs. AI systems deployed without adequate risk assessment, testing, and monitoring can fail in ways that cause significant harm — financial loss, discrimination, privacy breaches, or physical harm.

If a director-authorised AI system makes discriminatory credit decisions, causes a data breach, or produces incorrect medical outputs that harm patients, the directors who approved its deployment without adequate oversight may be held to have breached their duty of care.

Regulatory Exposure

AI intersects with a growing range of NZ regulatory frameworks:

Privacy Act 2020: AI systems that process personal information must comply with information privacy principles. Directors can be held responsible if the company deploys AI that systematically breaches privacy obligations.

Fair Trading Act 1986: AI-generated marketing claims, pricing algorithms, and personalised offers can create misleading conduct liability where the AI produces outputs that, while unintended, mislead consumers.

Employment Relations Act 2000: AI-assisted hiring, performance assessment, or dismissal processes raise significant fairness concerns. Directors who approve AI-driven HR processes without adequate human oversight face employment liability exposure.

Health and Disability Sector: AI in clinical decision support faces the most stringent requirements — directors of healthcare companies deploying diagnostic or treatment AI need specialist legal and governance advice.

Emerging Litigation Risk

International experience suggests AI-related director liability claims will increase as AI failures occur at scale. In the US and UK, class actions have already been filed against companies whose AI systems caused discriminatory or financially harmful outcomes. NZ litigation funders are actively monitoring these developments.

What Good AI Governance Looks Like for NZ Boards

The Institute of Directors' guidance on AI governance identifies several board-level responsibilities:

AI inventory and risk classification: Boards should know what AI systems their company deploys, the data they use, the decisions they support, and the potential harm if they fail or are misused.

Board-level AI expertise: Boards do not need technical AI experts — but they need directors who can ask the right questions and evaluate management's AI risk reporting critically. Skills audits should include AI literacy.

AI ethics and principles: Companies deploying AI should have an explicit AI ethics framework — setting out principles around fairness, transparency, human oversight, and prohibited uses — approved at board level.

Vendor due diligence: When AI is procured from a third party, the board should ensure management has conducted adequate due diligence on the vendor's governance, data practices, and liability position.

Incident response: AI systems can fail in novel and unexpected ways. Boards should ensure the company has an AI incident response plan — analogous to a cyber incident response plan — that they have reviewed.

Does D&O Insurance Cover AI-Related Claims?

Whether a D&O policy responds to an AI-related claim depends on the specific policy wording and the nature of the claim. Claims arising from directors' governance decisions around AI — approving deployment without adequate oversight, failing to respond to known AI risks — should fall within the standard D&O insuring clause.

However, claims that relate to the AI system's outputs causing direct harm (product liability) may fall outside D&O and require other coverage. Cyber liability policies may respond to privacy breaches caused by AI systems.

Boards should discuss AI-related D&O coverage with their broker to ensure there are no unexpected coverage gaps.

The Board's AI Governance Checklist

  • Has the board approved an AI governance framework or policy?
  • Does the board receive regular reporting on material AI deployments and their risk status?
  • Does the board have sufficient AI literacy to evaluate management's AI risk reporting?
  • Has the company conducted a risk assessment of its existing AI tools and systems?
  • Are customer-facing AI systems tested for bias, accuracy, and compliance before deployment?
  • Is there a clear accountability structure for AI decisions — including human override mechanisms?
  • Does the company's cyber and D&O insurance programme address AI-related liability?
