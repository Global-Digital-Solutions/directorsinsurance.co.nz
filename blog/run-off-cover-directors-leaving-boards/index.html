Run-Off Cover for Directors: What You Need to Know Before Leaving a Board

When a director leaves a board, their personal liability does not end. Run-off cover (tail cover) is essential — and many directors overlook it until it is too late.

By DirectorsInsurance Editorial·15 March 2026·5 min read

Why Leaving a Board Does Not End Your Liability

Directors often assume that when they resign from a board, their liability exposure ends. In reality, the opposite can be true — the period immediately after resignation is often when claims are most likely to emerge.

Claims arising from decisions made during your tenure as a director can be made years after you leave the board. Under the Limitation Act 2010, general civil claims must typically be brought within six years of the date the cause of action arose or the plaintiff discovered their loss. This means you can face a claim six years after you leave a board for a decision made during your last week of service.

How D&O Insurance Works: The Claims-Made Structure

D&O and management liability policies are "claims-made" policies. This means the policy responds to claims made during the policy period — not claims arising from acts that occurred during the policy period.

This distinction is critical. If you resigned from a board last year, and the current D&O policy lapses or is cancelled, a claim made today about a decision you made three years ago would not be covered — even though both the act and your tenure occurred before you left.

The Run-Off (Tail Cover) Solution

Run-off cover — also called tail cover — extends D&O coverage for a defined period after the policy terminates or after you leave a board. It covers claims made during the run-off period for acts committed prior to the policy's termination date.

Standard run-off periods in the NZ market are:

  • 3 years: Minimum recommended for low-risk boards
  • 6 years: Standard recommendation, reflecting the Limitation Act's primary limitation period
  • 7 years: Sometimes preferred for tax-related claims which can have longer limitation periods

Who Should Purchase Run-Off Cover?

When You Leave a Board

If you are resigning, retiring, or being removed from a board, you should:

  1. Check whether the company's existing D&O policy includes automatic run-off provisions for departing directors
  2. If not, request that the company purchase run-off cover on your behalf as a condition of your departure
  3. If the company refuses or cannot afford it, consider purchasing personal run-off cover independently

When a Company Is Sold or Changes Control

When a company is acquired, the acquirer typically replaces the D&O policy. Former directors who served under the old policy face a gap unless run-off cover is negotiated as part of the transaction. This is a standard M&A consideration that is sometimes overlooked.

When a Company Is Wound Up

If a company is being liquidated or wound up, all former directors should ensure run-off cover is in place. Liquidators are among the most common claimants against directors, and their investigations often take years — well beyond the date the company ceases to exist.

What Run-Off Cover Costs

Run-off cover is typically priced as a multiple of the expiring annual premium. In the NZ market:

  • 3-year run-off: Approximately 100–150% of the annual premium (paid as a one-time charge)
  • 6-year run-off: Approximately 150–250% of the annual premium (paid as a one-time charge)

For a company paying $5,000/year in D&O premium, 6-year run-off cover might cost $7,500–$12,500 as a one-time payment — often representing excellent value given the potential exposure.

Negotiating Run-Off on Your Way Out

If you are leaving a board and the company is maintaining a D&O policy for remaining directors, you can negotiate to have a run-off endorsement added to the existing policy specifically for your departure. Alternatively, some insurers offer personal run-off policies directly to departing directors.

Key negotiating points:

  • Insist on at least 6-year run-off
  • Ensure the run-off covers the same limits as the in-force policy
  • Confirm the run-off applies to claims by the company, liquidators, and third parties
  • Check that the run-off is not invalidated if the company changes insurers for its ongoing cover
