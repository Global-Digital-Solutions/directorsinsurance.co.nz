Trustee Liability: Are Charity Board Members Personally At Risk?

Many NZ charity trustees assume their volunteer status protects them from personal liability. It does not. Here is what charity board members need to know.

By DirectorsInsurance Editorial·20 March 2026·6 min read

The Volunteer Immunity Myth

A persistent myth among NZ charity trustees and board members is that volunteering for a charitable organisation shields them from personal liability. This is incorrect. New Zealand law imposes real duties on trustees and charity directors — and breaching those duties can result in personal financial liability, regardless of whether you are paid for your role.

The Legal Framework for Charity Trustees

The Charities Act 2005

The Charities Act 2005 requires charitable organisations registered with Charities Services to maintain proper governance, file annual returns, and apply their resources for charitable purposes only. Directors and trustees of registered charities who fail to comply with these requirements face deregistration and can be personally pursued for any misapplication of charitable funds.

The Incorporated Societies Act 2022

The Incorporated Societies Act 2022 (which replaced the 1908 Act) introduced formal officer duties for incorporated societies similar to those in the Companies Act. Officers of incorporated societies now have explicit duties to act in good faith, in the best interests of the society, and to exercise reasonable care, diligence, and skill. Personal liability for breach of these duties is expressly contemplated.

The Trusts Act 2019

Charitable trusts are also governed by the Trusts Act 2019, which codifies trustee duties including the duty to know the terms of the trust, to act in accordance with those terms, to act honestly and in good faith, and to act for the benefit of beneficiaries. Trustees who breach these duties can be personally liable to beneficiaries for losses.

Common Scenarios That Lead to Trustee Liability Claims

Misuse of charitable funds: Allowing charitable funds to be used for purposes outside the trust's purposes, or for the personal benefit of trustees, is a serious breach that can result in personal liability.

Insolvent trading by a charitable society: If a charitable incorporated society becomes insolvent and officers allow it to continue trading at creditors' expense, those officers face the same insolvent trading risks as company directors.

Employment disputes: Charities are employers, and employment claims — including personal grievance claims, discrimination allegations, and constructive dismissal claims — can name individual trustees and officers personally.

Regulatory action by Charities Services: Charities Services can investigate and deregister charities that fail to comply with their obligations. Deregistration can result in the charity's assets being redistributed, and individual trustees may face reputational and financial consequences.

What Insurance Do Charity Trustees Need?

Trustee Liability / Management Liability

Management liability insurance for charities and not-for-profit organisations covers:

  • Trustee/director liability: Personal protection for trustees against claims arising from governance decisions
  • Employment practices liability: Cover for employment claims from charity employees
  • Statutory liability: Cover for regulatory proceedings under the Charities Act and Incorporated Societies Act
  • Crime cover: Protection against fraud or theft by employees or volunteers

The Cost of Cover

Charity and not-for-profit management liability insurance is typically more affordable than equivalent corporate cover, reflecting the sector's risk profile. Many charities can obtain appropriate cover for NZ$600–$2,500 per year — a relatively small amount compared to the potential personal liability exposure.

Practical Governance Steps for Charity Trustees

  1. Ensure the charity has a current, accurate constitution that reflects its current activities
  2. Hold regular board meetings with proper minutes
  3. Maintain accurate financial records and file annual returns with Charities Services on time
  4. Ensure all fund expenditure is clearly within the charity's stated purposes
  5. Obtain independent legal advice for any significant governance decisions
  6. Maintain management liability insurance covering all trustees
  7. Ensure run-off cover is obtained when trustees leave the board
